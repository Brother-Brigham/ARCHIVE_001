## **Part 1: Your Design**

- What type of pattern or practice did you create?  
    (rosary-style, mala-style, misbaha-style, or personal/adapted)
I went with a Catholic-style rosary.
- How did you structure it?  
    (number of beads, sections, repetitions)
I was initially just going to use beads for each Hail Mary, Our Father, and the Cross, but tying knots to separate each was too time-consuming, so I used additional beads to divide the parts. In total, I used one cross, 53 black beads for each Hail Mary, six white beads for the Our Fathers, and twelve olive wood beads to divide all of the sections.
- Why did you choose this design?
I chose this design because it seemed like a simple, reverent, and effective way to interact with sacred time.

## **Part 3: Observing Repetition**

- What repeated in your design?
There was deliberate patterning in my design. The main body of the rosary was 10 Hail Mary beads, then a separator bead, then an Our Father bead, then a separator bead, then another 10 Hail Mary beads. The bead colour and shape distinctions for each kind of bead was also repeated for consistency.
- Did the repetition create a rhythm or pattern? How?
The repetition did create a pattern. By keeping the beads organized by colour and shape, I establish a pattern where all of the Hail Mary bead are one type, all of the Our Fathers another, and all of the separator beads another. The pattern helps meaningfully differentiate the sections of the rosary so that it can be used properly.
- Did anything change as you repeated the pattern over time?
As I repeated the pattern over time, I found myself able to make the repetitions faster, more skillfully, and appreciated the process more than when I first started out.

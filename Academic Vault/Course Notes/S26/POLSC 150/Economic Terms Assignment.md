**Terms/Concepts**:

1. Political Economy: How politics drives economics, and vice versa (they simply don't exist without each other)
Canadian politics directly influence the market by managing production of certain agricultural goods through quotas. This political management of the economy keeps prices stable for producers, but differs from the system in the US that includes programs that subsidize certain farm operations. https://www.canada.ca/en/farm-products-council/services/supply-management.html
2. GDP: Gross Domestic Product. The total dollar value of all _goods_ and _services_ produced in a country. 
As of 2024, Canada's GDP was 2.24 trillion dollars. This is about ten times lower than the US's 28.75 trillion dollar GDP. https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.CD?locations=US-CA
3. GDP per capita: GDP divided by how many people live in a country (it's a rough estimate of how much money a person in that country makes)
The per-capita difference in GDP is much less drastic. Canada has a GDP per capita of $54,340.3, compared to the US's 84,534 GDP per capita as of 2024. https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.PCAP.CD?locations=CA-US
4. GDP at PPP: GDP when adapted for cost of living
When adjusted for PPP, the difference remains very similar to the unadjusted GDP. Canada sits at $2.67 trillion as of 2024, with the US at $29.18 trillion in the same year. https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NY.GDP.MKTP.PP.CD?locations=CA-US
5. Property: anything a person can own is property
When considered in terms of land ownership, property in Canada is overwhelmingly owned by the government: as mush as 89% of Canadian land is "Crown Land", or public land owned by either the federal or provincial governments. In the US, a much greater amount of land is owned by private citizens and organizations. https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/crown-land
6. Market (supply and demand): how much there is of something (supply) and how much people want it (demand) determine how much something costs.
The recent supply and demand curve in Canada has driven housing costs to extreme highs nationwide, but a number of factors present going into 2026 may reduce costs for homeownership--among those causes is a lack of population growth, which reduces the demand for homes and thus decreases pressure on supply. The US has seen similar housing crises before, but not on as much of a nationwide scale in comparison to Canada's. https://www.cmhc-schl.gc.ca/professionals/housing-markets-data-and-research/market-reports/housing-market/housing-market-outlook
7. Comparative Advantage: when a country can produce something more cost-efficiently than another country
Canada produces about 75% of the world's maple syrup supply. The only other nation that competes with Canada at commercial scale is the United States, which makes up the remaining 25% of maple syrup production. https://agriculture.canada.ca/en/sector/horticulture/reports/statistical-overview-canadian-maple-industry-2025
8. Agriculture (economic sector): being paid to do farming
Canada's western provinces, namely Alberta, Saskatchewan, and Manitoba, are the major agricultural producers in the country. This is similar to the many states throughout the midwestern United States that rely on agriculture as their primary economic sector. https://agriculture.canada.ca/en/sector/crops/reports-statistics/canada-outlook-principal-field-crops-2026-05-21
9. Industry (economic sector): being paid to _make_ some physical thing (factories). 
Canada's automotive industry is one of the largest contributors to Canada's GDP, providing $16.8 billion in 2024. This is comparable to the United States' automotive industry, which is also a large producer of revenue and jobs. https://ised-isde.canada.ca/site/ised/en/research-and-business-intelligence/industry-sector-intelligence/manufacturing-industries/canadian-automotive-industry
10. Services (economic sector): being paid to _do_ something. 
Canada's banking seems to be much more centralized than banking is in the United States. Banking is also a significant economic sector in Canada, reportedly contributing $74 billion to Canada's GDP. https://cba.ca/article/contributing-to-the-economy
11. Post-industrialism: when a countries economy is primarily made of up "services". 
According to the CIA World Factbook, Canada's economy as of 2000 was made up primarily of services, at 66% of the economy. The US economy seemed comparable based on CIA World Factbook data, with services making up 72.5% of the US economy. https://user.iiasa.ac.at/~marek/fbook/01/geos/ca.html
12. Alternative economic measurements (HDI, Happiness): it's NOT all about the Benjamin's: there are other ways to measure how well a countries population is doing.
According to the Human Development Index, Canada and the US are remarkably similar in HDI ranking. The US comes in at 17th place, with a score of 0.938, and Canada comes in at 16th place, with a score of 0.939. https://hdr.undp.org/data-center/specific-country-data#/countries/CAN

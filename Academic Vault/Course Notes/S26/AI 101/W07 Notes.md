## 📜 Unit 3 Reading: The Internal Governance Crisis & The Palestinian Schism

### The Definition of Statehood and the Monopoly on Force

To understand why a Palestinian state does not exist today, political scientists cannot look solely at external factors like Israeli military policy or international vetoes; we must also look at the internal structural requirements of statehood itself. In classical sociology, Max Weber famously defined a state as a human community that successfully claims the **"monopoly on the legitimate use of physical force"** within a given territory. For a state to function, interact with the international community, and enforce treaties, it must have a single, unified government and a centralized military or police command.

Today, Palestinian governance is fundamentally fractured, lacking this precise monopoly. Instead, Palestinian politics is locked in a bitter, multi-decade geographical and ideological duopoly. The territory is split into two separate administrative zones ruled by fiercely antagonistic factions: **Fatah** (which administers parts of the West Bank via the Palestinian Authority) and **Hamas** (the Islamist militant group that controls the Gaza Strip). This internal civil conflict has effectively paralyzed the Palestinian national movement, leaving it without a single, unified diplomatic voice capable of negotiating a binding peace treaty or managing a cohesive sovereign state.

### The Genesis of the Divide: The 2006 Legislative Crisis

The structural breakdown of Palestinian governance traces back to a pivotal democratic exercise that yielded catastrophic geopolitical consequences. Following the death of longtime Palestinian Liberation Organization (PLO) leader Yasser Arafat, his successor, Mahmoud Abbas of the secular Fatah party, was elected President of the Palestinian Authority (PA) in 2005. Founded under the Oslo Accords, the PA was designed to be a secular, transitional government that explicitly recognized Israel's right to exist and committed to a negotiated two-state solution.

However, in January 2006, the PA held legislative elections. To the shock of Fatah and international observers, **Hamas won a majority of seats** in the Palestinian Legislative Council. Hamas, founded in 1987 as an offshoot of the Muslim Brotherhood, possessed a charter that openly called for the destruction of Israel and rejected the Oslo Accords entirely.

A volatile constitutional crisis immediately erupted. The "Quartet on the Middle East" (the United States, Russia, the European Union, and the United Nations) stated that international aid to the new Hamas-led government would be frozen unless Hamas agreed to three non-negotiable conditions:

1. Renounce violence and terrorism.
    
2. Recognize the state of Israel.
    
3. Accept all previous diplomatic agreements (including the Oslo Accords).
    

Hamas refused to accept these conditions, viewing them as a surrender of their core ideological platform.

### The 2007 Civil War and Territorial Bifurcation

The political gridlock rapidly degenerated into physical warfare. In June 2007, after months of mounting tensions and failed attempts to form a unity government, violent clashes erupted between armed Fatah loyalists and Hamas fighters. This brief but bloody conflict—often referred to as the Palestinian Civil War or the Battle of Gaza—resulted in a total territorial divorce.

Hamas forces violently seized complete military and administrative control of the Gaza Strip, expelling PA security forces. In response, President Mahmoud Abbas declared a state of emergency, dissolved the Hamas-led coalition government, and dismissed Hamas Prime Minister Ismail Haniyeh. Abbas then appointed a new cabinet based in Ramallah, exercising civil authority over the isolated Palestinian enclaves in the West Bank.

This 2007 split created a profound **territorial and political bifurcation**:

- **The West Bank (Fatah/PA):** Run by the internationally recognized, secular Palestinian Authority. It cooperates heavily with Israeli military forces on security coordination to suppress Hamas cells within the West Bank and relies on Western financial aid.
    
- **The Gaza Strip (Hamas):** Run as a de facto militant enclave by Hamas. Because Hamas rejects coexistence with Israel and maintains an armed wing (the Al-Qassam Brigades), Israel and Egypt imposed a strict land, air, and sea blockade on Gaza following the 2007 takeover to prevent the import of heavy weaponry.
    

### The Governance Crisis: Legitimacy and "Divide and Conquer"

This factional divide has severely eroded the internal legitimacy of Palestinian governance. President Mahmoud Abbas is currently serving the 21st year of what was originally elected as a four-year term; no presidential elections have been held since 2005, and no legislative elections since 2006. The PA suffers from rampant corruption, economic stagnation, and a deep crisis of domestic authority, with many Palestinians viewing it not as a state-in-waiting, but as an administrative sub-contractor of the Israeli occupation.

Furthermore, global analysts note that this internal fracture has been heavily exploited by external actors. Mainstream Israeli security perspectives argue that Israel cannot realistically grant statehood when a major portion of the territory is ruled by an armed group dedicated to its erasure, asserting that any withdrawal from the West Bank would simply create a second "militant launchpad" identical to Gaza.

Conversely, Palestinian and regional critics argue that right-wing Israeli leadership historically favored this split, operating under a strategic doctrine of "divide and conquer." By keeping Fatah weak in Ramallah and allowing Hamas to maintain its grip on Gaza, Israeli hardliners could plausibly claim to the international community that there was "no unified partner for peace," thereby indefinitely freezing the peace process while continuing to build settlements.

- **Islam:** Known as the **Noble Sanctuary** (_Al-Haram al-Sharif_), it is the third-holiest site in Islam. It houses the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, marking the spot where Muslims believe the Prophet Muhammad ascended to heaven during the Night Journey.

When secular political goals are re-framed as religious obligations, compromise becomes a sin. Both Jewish and Islamic religious movements have used theology to delegitimize the other's right to the land:

Under this theological framework, no leader, king, or president has the authority to cede an inch of Palestinian land to a non-Muslim power. This completely closes the door to a negotiated two-state solution, turning diplomatic give-and-take into a holy war (_Jihad_).

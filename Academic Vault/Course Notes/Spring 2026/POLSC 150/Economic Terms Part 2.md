1. Economic System Spectrum: Market Economy v. Command Economy: How much influence does the gov't have in the economic system
The Canadian economy is primarily a market economy, but does have some significant command economy elements due to government regulations. The US takes a more Market Economy approach by comparison, but does exercise a bit of command through regulations such as anti-monopoly laws. https://cdhowe.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/Commentary_382_0-1-20241220-040629.pdf
2. Free Market/Laissez Faire (system): the gov't has little to no influence in the economy
Generally, Canada's market is a free market, similar to the US's. https://competition-bureau.canada.ca/en/how-we-foster-competition/consultations/abuse-dominance-enforcement-guidelines
3. Capitalism (system): an economic system that focuses heavily on creating wealth/making a profit. Interestingly, not limited to free-market systems.
Canada's banking sector and the Toronto Stock Exchange are examples of capitalism at work in Canada. These function similarly to the US's banking sector and stock markets. https://www.bis.org/mc/currency_areas/ca.htm 
4. Socialism (system): an economic system where the gov't taxes and spends heavily. Can still be a free-market system. 
Canada taxes heavily in order to sustain a socialistic, universal healthcare system. Though similar in some respects to the US's Medicare/Medicade programs, funding it requires a much heavier tax than is used in the US. In addition, the US healthcare system is very different based on the practical need to have some kind of private healthcare insurance. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/canada-health-care-system.html
5. Communism (system): where the gov't owns/controls the entire system. 
Cuba functions as a communist economic system. Under this system, the government dictates how much and of what kind of property the citizens can receive, but the property is still ultimately owned by the state. This is drastically different from the US, where private property is a core concept to the American system and ideal. Somewhat recently, though, Cuba was beginning to soften and allow for a greater number of private ventures than in the past. https://www.britannica.com/place/Cuba/Demographic-trends#ref54401
6. Monopoly: when one business controls the entire market for one good/service. 
In Canada, Ontario's Liquor Control Act has created a government-mandated/controlled monopoly on the sale of liquor in the province. Generally in the US, liquor sales are broadly done by a number of organizations. https://cdhowe.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/Commentary_382_0-1-20241220-040629.pdf
7. Central Bank: a national bank whose primary task is to manage the monetary system of a country (i.e. how much currency is worth). 
The Bank of Canada was set up to be Canada's central bank. In practice, it functions very similarly to the US Federal Reserve. https://www.bis.org/mc/currency_areas/ca.htm
8. Inflation: the price of goods and services goes up.
Inflation seems to be a regular occurrence, but 2022 saw a massive inflation rate of 6.8%. The US has also seen a lot of inflation over the last few years, likely as a result of COVID-related supply chain issues. https://www.statsnwt.ca/prices-expenditures/cpi/historical_cpi/index.html
9. Tariff: an additional tax on goods or services purchased from another country (officially paid by the country, but often just paid for by consumers)
Canada has been using tariffs to prevent an influx of Chinese electric vehicles from completely upturning the domestic vehicle manufacturers' place in the market. Similarly, the US has been using tariffs to attempt to control trade since the beginning of Trump's second term. https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/chinese-made-evs-arrive-canada-9.7219356
10. Quota: when the gov't limits the number of goods or services that can be imported from another country. 
In the previous example, Canada's tariff agreement also places a quota of 24,500 cars every six months. It seems likely to me that the US does not enforce such quotas, preferring a greater emphasis on free market principles. https://www.cbc.ca/news/politics/chinese-made-evs-arrive-canada-9.7219356
11. Subsidies: when the gov't pays certain industries so they can sell their goods or services for less. 
As one example, the Canadian gov't is providing subsidies to certain auto manufacturers to assist with EV battery manufacturing. The US government subsidizes a number of industries, such as the dairy industry, through government programs. https://distribution-a617274656661637473.pbo-dpb.ca/243471bcfade3decfc9a60ed9050b6438ccf5dca3db091709abb68793915ddeb
12. Public Goods: gov't spending that benefits everyone
Canada's armed forces qualify as a public good. This is just like how the US's armed forces are also a public good. https://www.canada.ca/en/services/defence/caf.html
13. Social Expenditure: gov't spending that benefits people that meet certain criteria (usually people in lower socio-economic categories)
The Canada Child Benefit (CCB) program is a social expenditure that provides additional income to caretakers of children. It is similar to the US Child Tax Credit. https://www.canada.ca/en/revenue-agency/programs/about-canada-revenue-agency-cra/income-statistics-gst-hst-statistics/canada-child-benefit-statistics/canada-child-benefit-statistics-2021-tax-year.html
14. Freedom (economic): the right to do what ever we want
Canada is built on a principle of economic freedom much like the US's foundation of economic freedom: individuals have the right to choose professions, start private businesses, own and sell property, etc. https://competition-bureau.canada.ca/en/how-we-foster-competition/consultations/abuse-dominance-enforcement-guidelines
15. Equality (economic): everyone gets the same of something, usually an outcome or an opportunity.
Canada's equalization program redistributes wealth from the wealthiest provinces to the poorer provinces, so that citizens across the country can receive similar access to public services. The US does not have any formalized equalization program in law. https://www.canada.ca/en/department-finance/programs/federal-transfers/equalization.html

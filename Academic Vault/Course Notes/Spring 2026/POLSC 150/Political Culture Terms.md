**Terms**:

1. Political Culture: how Societal Institutions drive political _and_ economic behavior/decision-making/policy-making among individuals and groups. 
Canada's political culture is built around a fairly communitarian ideal of "peace, order, and good government". This is in contrast to the US's ideal of "life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness". https://thecanadianencyclopedia.ca/en/article/political-culture
2. Political/Economic Ideology: What should government do vis-a-vis its constituents?
    1. Traditional Liberal Ideology: Government should generally stay out of constituent behavior
Canada passed a federal law, legalizing cannabis use for people 18 and older with minimal restrictions. This kind of law is an example of the government allowing people to do more independently, and contrasts the more varied legal landscape in the US regarding cannabis legislation. https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/cj-jp/cannabis/
    2. Traditional Conservative Ideology: Government should have a lot of influence on constituent behavior
The Canadian government regulates broadcasting of content to include a certain amount of Canadian content, in an effort to maintain Canadian culture. The US does not require this, as far as I can tell. https://crtc.gc.ca/eng/cancon/r_cdn.htm
3. Political/Economic Attitudes: Direction and speed of political change
    1. Radical Attitude: liberalizing fast
After implementing Medical Assistance in Dying (MAID) in 2016, Canada has since rapidly expanded on those laws. The US, on the other hand, generally maintains tight restrictions on assisted suicide. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/health-services-benefits/medical-assistance-dying/legislation-canada.html
    2. Liberal Attitude: liberalizing slowly
Canada's multiculturalism laws developed more slowly over a period of decades. The US has seen a similar attitude in the development of civil rights law throughout US history. https://lop.parl.ca/sites/PublicWebsite/default/en_CA/ResearchPublications/200920E
	3. Conservative Attitude: status quo, or becoming conservative slowly
Canada's keeping the British monarch as Head of State is an example of conservative attitude, the opposite of the US's revolution against the British monarch and establishment of an American Head of State. https://www.canada.ca/en/canadian-heritage/services/crown-canada/monarch.html
	4. Reactionary Attitude: becoming more conservative fast
Canada froze the bank accounts of citizens engaged in the "Freedom Convoy" protests in 2022, exercising national emergency powers to silence the protests quickly. The US very rarely engages with domestic protesters in a comparable way, if ever. https://publicorderemergencycommission.ca/files/documents/Final-Report/Vol-1-Report-of-the-Public-Inquiry-into-the-2022-Public-Order-Emergency.pdf
4. Citizenship: an individual is officially recognized as belonging to a state/country, with all its privileges _and_ duties. 
Canada's citizenship system is a civic citizenship system, granting citizenship to those who are able to civically align themselves with Canadian law and culture and swear an oath to the crown. In addition, and similarly to the US, Canada grants birthright citizenship to anyone born on Canadian soil. https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship.html
5. Individualism: When individuals within society care more about their own wellbeing than the wellbeing of society as a whole
The Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms offers protections to individual rights and liberties, similarly to the individual protections in the Bill of Rights and other US Constitutional amendments. Protecting individual rights and liberties at a federal level preserves a degree of individualism. https://www.justice.gc.ca/eng/csj-sjc/rfc-dlc/ccrf-ccdl/rfcp-cdlp.html
6. Collectivism: When individuals within society care more about the wellbeing of society as a whole than their own wellbeing.
Canada's universal healthcare system is an example of collectivism. This largely contrasts with the US's privatized healthcare system, though some collectivist elements exist in the US thanks to Medicare/Medicaid. https://www.canada.ca/en/health-canada/services/health-care-system/canada-health-care-system-medicare/canada-health-act.html
7. Freedom (political culture): the right to do what ever we want
Canada is among the highest ranking nations in terms of freedom, per Freedom House. The United States ranks lower on the Freedom House Index. https://freedomhouse.org/country/canada
8. Equality (political culture): everyone gets the same of something, usually an outcome or an opportunity. 
Canada implements Employment Equity legislation, mandating that women, Indigenous peoples, and members of minorities be given equal representation in the workplace. This is comparable to DEI and Equal Employment Opportunity legislation that has been present in the US. https://laws-lois.justice.gc.ca/eng/acts/e-5.401/

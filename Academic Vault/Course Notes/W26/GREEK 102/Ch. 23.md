## 23α Story
![[Pasted image 20260316121412.png]]
And the army of the Peloponnesians advancing arrived in Attica first at Oenoe, where they intended to invade. And when they encamped, they prepared to make attacks on the wall with both siege engines and otherwise; for Oenoe, being on the borders of Attica and Boeotia, had been fortified with a wall and the Athenians used it as a garrison whenever war occurred. And so they prepared attacks and they spent time around it in vain. And Archidamus received no small blame from this; for the Athenians brought in all in this time.
![[Pasted image 20260316121444.png]]
When they had attacked Oenoe and tried every way but were not able to take it, and the Athenians were not sending ambassadors to make peace proposals, then they set out from Oenoe and invaded Attica; and Archidamus, king of the Lacedaemonians, was lead.
![[Pasted image 20260316121512.png]]
And they, camping, they first ravaged Eleusis and the Thriasian plain. And after they advanced until they arrived at Acharnae, the biggest district in Attica of the so-called demes, and encamping themselves there they set camp and remaining there a long time were ravaging. And it is said that Archidamus was of such opinion that he stayed posting around Acharnae preparing for battle, and they would not have to go down the plain to battle them; for he was hoping the Athenians would march out against them and would not overlook that their land was being ravaged.
![[Pasted image 20260316121543.png]]
So when they went not to meet him at Eleusis and the Thriasian plain, he encamped at Acharnae and he tested whether they would come out against him; for at the same time the district seemed suitable to him to encamp in, and at the same time he thought the Acharnians, being a large part of the city (for they were three thousand hoplites), would not overlook the fact that their own property was being destroyed, but would urge on everyone to fight.

## Pericles
![[Pasted image 20260323113811.png]]
Saying this, Pericles tried to rid the Athenians of their anger against him. And on the one hand they publicly obeyed his words and sent no more ambassadors to the Lacedaemonians and they were in a state of eagerness, but on the other hand they were privately grieved by their sufferings. But they did not stop holding him in anger until they fined him for money. And not much later they chose him to be a general again and entrusted all their matters {to him}.
